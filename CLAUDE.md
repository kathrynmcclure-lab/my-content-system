## Voice and tone

Voice is consistent. Tone is variable.
Voice is personality. Tone is mood.
Voice is concrete, evergreen, and routine. Tone changes with context.

### Our voice
Ox & Adder consumers are smart, savvy, and tuned in. They see their savings as daily wins worthy of small celebrations, and they prefer communication that is straightforward, clear, and positive. Ox & Adder's voice is human and friendly. When speaking to our consumers we never want to be overbearing, childish, or too coach-y.

When we write content we're:
- **Playful** - We don't take ourselves too seriously. While we prioritize clarity over entertainment, we're not afraid to use a pun or quip from time to time.
- **Smart** - We want to help our customers reach their goals as quickly and efficiently as possible. Our writing is action-driven, positive, and precise.
- **Helpful** - We write as if we're speaking to a friend or coworker. We use simple sentence structures and plain language that anyone can understand.
- **Practical** - We understand that every interaction is one touchpoint in an ongoing conversation. We establish trust by being polite and direct.

### Our tone
Ox & Adder's tone is friendly and positive by default. We want to keep things fun and informal, but we don't want to be overly clever and miss out on clarity. Ox & Adder has a sense of humor when appropriate, but we don't go out of our way to make a joke unless it flows naturally. We use the language our consumers use, staying away from big words and making technical stuff as clear as we can.

When writing content we should be:
- Informal and conversational, but not too casual or trendy.
- Smart and trustworthy, but not overly complex or pleading.
- Optimistic and playful, but not overly silly or clever.
- Action-oriented and helpful, but not pushy or micromanaging.

## General writing guidance

- Use active voice — the subject of the sentence does the action.
- Use the first person (we/our) when talking about Ox & Adder.
- Use the second person (you/your) when talking about our consumers or clients.
- Use the third person (he/she/it/they) when talking about a brand, offer, or store.
- Focus your message and lead with the main point.
- Be concise without sacrificing clarity.
- Be specific and avoid vague language.
- Be consistent with copy patterns and style points.
- Read your writing out loud and listen for anything that sounds out of place.
- Use Hemingway app: grade 6 or below for D2C audiences, grade 10 or below for B2B audiences.
- Use plain language when possible:
  - For D2C: completely avoid technical jargon, business speak, Ox & Adder internal vernacular, and fancy words.
  - For B2B: incorporate technical terminology to match the mental models and language of the space.

## Grammar, mechanics, and style

If something is not listed in this style guide, default to the AP Stylebook.

### Numbers, dates, and time

#### Dates
- For short-form copy and titles, keep dates short to drive urgency, clarity, and scannability.
- Always use two-digit values for the year in date format.
- For titles and headlines, it's acceptable to spell out the month and use ordinals for the date.
- If abbreviating a written date, use the first 3 letters of the day and month.
- For long-form copy (emails, webpages, etc.):
  - Include the weekday before the date.
  - Use 4-digit values for the year.
  - Don't use ordinals for the date when accompanied by the year.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Ends 1/24/17 | Ends Saturday, January 24th (product body copy) |
| Offer ends on 3/5/24 | Offer ends on 03/05/24 |
| Join us Tuesday, June 18, 2024 | Join us Tuesday, June 18th, 2024 (long-form body copy) |
| Easter bonus available April 1st (title copy) | Easter bonus available April 1 |

#### Decimals
- Round to no more than one decimal (nearest tenth) in tables, graphics, and sales collateral.
- Avoid decimals in sentences; round to the nearest whole number.

#### Money
- For whole dollar amounts in sentences and headers, do not use a decimal unless referring to a cash back offer.
- Use the dollar sign before the amount.
- For amounts less than $1, use a 0 before the decimal.
- Cash back amounts are always formatted as $0.00.

Examples: `$0.75` / `$10` / `$19.99` / `$1.00 cash back`

#### Numbers in body copy
- Use numerals in all cases, except at the beginning of sentences.
- Spell out a number when it begins a sentence and doesn't refer to a cash back offer.
- Numbers over 3 digits get commas.

Examples: `One app for all your favorite stores & websites` / `Get 5% cash back` / `999 / 1,000 / 150,000`

#### Numbers in headlines
- Capitalize only the first word of your headline.
- If the headline begins with a number, do not capitalize the second word unless it is a proper noun.
- If the headline begins with a number, use numerals for all numbers.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| 3 ways to write headlines | 3 Ways to write headlines / Three ways to write headlines |
| $5 Uber rider bonus | $5 uber rider bonus |
| 2 for 1 deal available! | 2 for one deal available! |

#### Percentages
Use the % symbol instead of spelling out "percent."

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Get 5% cash back | Get 5 percent cash back |

#### Ranges and spans
Use a hyphen (-) to indicate a range or span of numbers.

Examples: `It takes 20-30 days.` / `Your withdrawal should be available in 1-3 hours.`

#### Ordinals and sequences
Spell out ordinals and sequences in most body copy. Exceptions can be made for titles/headlines.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Get $25 for referring your fifth friend! | Get $25 for referring your 5th friend! |
| It's Your 5th Ox & Adderversary! (email title) | It's your 5th Ox & Adderversary! (body copy) |

#### Time
- Use numerals and AM or PM, with a space in between.
- Don't use minutes for on-the-hour time.
- Use a hyphen between times to indicate a time period.

Examples: `7 AM` / `7:30 PM` / `7 AM - 10:30 PM`

#### Time zones
Specify time zones when writing about an event. Use 2-letter abbreviations:
- Eastern time: ET
- Central time: CT
- Mountain time: MT
- Pacific time: PT

Example: `Bonus eligible on purchases made before 7 AM MT on 3/5/24.`

### Punctuation

#### Ampersands
- Use ampersands (&) instead of "and" in categories. Write out "and" fully everywhere else.
- If an ampersand appears in a list item, do not use the serial comma.
- In short copy, sometimes acceptable to use an ampersand (check with Content Designer or Copywriter first).

#### Apostrophes
- Use only as contractions or to show possession, never to create a plural noun.
- Plural noun possession: apostrophe at the end (`influencers'`).
- Singular noun ending in s: add 's (`Chris's`).

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| We appreciate all of our Savers. | We appreciate all of our Saver's. |
| Rich signed up with Colin's referral code. | Rich signed up with Colins' referral code. |
| Rich signed up with Chris's referral code. | Rich signed up with Chris' referral code. |

#### Commas
- Use a comma to indicate a natural breath when reading aloud.
- More than 2 commas in a single non-list sentence? Restructure into two sentences.
- Use the serial comma (Oxford comma) in lists.
- Don't use the serial comma if an ampersand (&) is used in the category.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Earn a bonus when shopping on Jet, Uber, and Boxed | Earn a bonus when shopping on Jet, Uber and Boxed |
| Beer, wine & spirits | Beer, wine, and spirits |

#### Contractions
Use them, and more often than not. They give writing a casual, friendly tone.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| You'll earn cash back automatically within 24 hours | You will earn cash back automatically within 24 hours |
| We can't wait to show you these new offers. | We cannot wait to show you these new offers. |

#### Exclamation points
- Use sparingly, never more than one at a time, or twice in a single paragraph.
- Reserve for truly celebratory user moments in shorter/product copy.
- Never use in failure messages or alerts.
- Go inside quotation marks.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| We found cash! | Request timed out! |
| You earned cash back! | Earn cash back on Halloween candy! Shop now! |

#### Hyphens and dashes

**Hyphens (-)**
- Link words into a single phrase, or indicate a span or range. No spaces on either side.
- Hyphenated words are adjectives modifying a word that comes after them.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| She was a first-time user. | She was a first-time-user. |
| We're available Monday-Friday. | — |
| It's time for back-to-school shopping. | It's time for back to school shopping. |
| It's time to go back to school. | It's time to go back-to-school. |

**Em dash (—)**
- Use in place of a semicolon or to offset an aside.
- Shortcut: option + shift + -
- Always add a space on either side.

Examples:
- `Tis the season for cash back — earn on decorations, festive attire, and more!`
- `The featured screen — one of our new features — is where you'll find personalized recommendations.`

#### Periods
- Use at the end of all body copy, error messages, activity notifications, push notifications, confirmations, empty states, and lists.
- Don't use in headlines or subheadlines.
- Only use in a list if each entry forms a full sentence.

#### Semicolons and colons
Avoid generally — they usually support long, complicated sentences that could be simplified. Use an em dash or start a new sentence instead.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Save 50% with code YAYSAVE50 | Save 50% with code: YAYSAVE50 |

### Formatting

#### Capitalization

**Title case** — Capitalize first letter of every word except articles, prepositions, and conjunctions.
- Used for products, bonuses, offers, categories, and navigational elements in the app.

Examples: `Ox & Adder Days` / `New Offers` / `Your Earnings` / `Earn More` / `Switch & Save`

**Sentence case** — Capitalize only the first letter of the first word.
- Used for in-app copy: sentences, headings, labels, titles, buttons, etc.
- Ox & Adder Care communications always use sentence case.

Examples: `Earning cash back is easy when you shop with Ox & Adder` / `Shop now` / `Want to stop missing out on cash back?`

#### Categories
Use sentence case and ampersands.

Examples: `Grocery` / `Online shopping` / `Beauty & wellness`

#### Display tables
- Order content so the most important information is first.
- Labels should be concise (no more than 2-3 words) and follow a consistent part of speech/tense.
- Data text should fit on 1 line, but can wrap to 2 lines if necessary.

#### Headlines and subheadlines
- Use sentence case for both.
- Should be hierarchical, descending in importance and font size.
- No closing punctuation.
- Avoid multi-sentence headlines.
- If an action is required, explain what the action is and why users should take it.
- Headlines should match the CTAs on the page.

Examples: `Earn on your entire purchase, instantly` / `We found cash!` / `What else did you buy?`

#### Links
- Use links to direct to a document or digital location outside of a particular flow or the app.
- Stand-alone links: start with a motivating verb, be concise and specific.
- Don't say "Click here!" or "Click for more information" or "Read this."
- In-line links: don't link the punctuation mark at the end of a sentence or before a comma.

#### Lists
- Always capitalize the first word of a list.
- Don't use punctuation in simple lists (3 or fewer words per item) or if items don't form complete sentences.
- If one list item is a complete sentence, use a period at the end of all items.
- Use numbered lists if order matters; bulleted lists if not.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Grocery / Online shopping / Beer, wine & spirits | Grocery. / Online shopping. / Beer, wine & spirits. |

#### Text formatting

**All caps, italics, or underlining:** Do not use anywhere in product or marketing copy.

**Bold:** Use when citing an in-app element, or referencing button/navigation labels in step-by-step instructions. Where text formatting isn't possible, add quotation marks around navigational elements.

Examples: `...tap Submit receipt to take pictures.` / `Tap Your list to see your offers` / `Tap "Your list" to see your offers`

**Line spacing:** Leave one space between sentences, never two.

### People, places, and things

#### Pronouns

**First person (we/us):** Use when speaking from the perspective of Ox & Adder or its employees. Never use "I" or "me" to refer to Ox & Adder or the end-consumer. Don't use "we" or "our" when referring to client products or cash back offers.

**Second person (you/your):** Use when referring to a collective audience or individual consumer. Never use "me" or "my."

**Third person (he/she/they/it):** Use "they/them/their" as a singular pronoun when gender is unknown or irrelevant. Never use "one" as a pronoun. Use "they/them" when referring to a company or group.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Clorox has new spring cleaning products! | We have new spring cleaning products. |
| Your Earnings | My Earnings |

#### Individuals
- Marketing: first mention = first and last name; subsequent mentions = first name only.
- IPN: first mention = first name, last name, job title, and company; subsequent mentions = last name.

Examples:
- `Maddie Groves is a Senior Manager on the Marketing team at Ox & Adder.` (then: "Maddie")
- `Bryan Leach, CEO and Founder of Ox & Adder` (IPN first reference)

#### Titles
- Ubiquitous titles can be abbreviated on first reference; lesser-known titles should be spelled out.
- Capitalize individual job titles when referencing a specific role.
- Don't capitalize job titles when referring to the role in general terms.

Examples: `Our new Marketing Manager starts today.` vs. `All the managers use Ox & Adder when they purchase supplies.`

#### Groups
When referencing individuals in a group, list names alphabetically unless there's a specific reason for a different order.

#### Quotes
- Use the present tense in testimonials, social/blog posts, or other publications.
- For user testimonials, write first name and first initial of last name followed by a period (e.g., `Sarah S.`).

#### Companies, Departments, and Teams

**Companies:** Use a common-sense approach to present the company name recognizably and accurately. When in doubt, check the company's website.

Examples: `nutpods` (lowercase 'n' per company website) / `General Mills` (drop "Inc.")

**Departments and teams:** Capitalize department and team names, but not "team" or "department."

Examples: `Marketing team` / `Care department`

#### States and cities
- Spell out all city and state names.
- Per AP Style, all cities should be accompanied by their state, with exceptions for major cities (Atlanta, Los Angeles, New York).
- If a city is mentioned in geographically targeted comms, leave off the state.

#### URLs and websites
- Use all lowercase.
- Avoid spelling out http://www.

Examples: `ox&adder.com` / `amazon.com` / `ceo@ox&adder.com`

### Perspective

#### Active voice
Use active voice. Avoid passive voice. "Was" and "by" may indicate passive voice.

One exception: when you want to emphasize the action over the subject.

Examples:
- Active: `Cara earned cash back.`
- Passive (acceptable): `Your receipt was flagged by our fraud team.`

### Style

#### Abbreviations and acronyms
- Avoid abbreviations when possible; spell out words or phrases fully when space allows.
- Types: Acronyms (pronounced as a word), Initialisms (each letter pronounced), Shortened words.
- Spell out abbreviations on first reference with the acronym in parentheses.
- If the first mention is in a heading, use the acronym and spell it out in supporting text.
- Some terms (PDF, API) are more recognizable as acronyms — acceptable to use on their own.
- Only use periods with shortened words that aren't pluralized: `imp.` (impression) / `imps.` (impressions).

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| Increasing funded active redeemers (FARs)... This quarter, FARs have already increased 6%. | Increasing FARs is one of our major objectives. |
| APIs | APIes or API's |

#### Copyrights and trademarks
Avoid including ®, ™, or © symbols in marketing communications when possible.

#### Emoji
Use with purpose and intention, sparingly. Never use an emoji to replace a word.

| Yes | No |
|-----|-----|
| You earned $2.50! 🎉 | 🎉🙌🤑 You earned $2.50! 🤑🙌🎉 |
| Make some dough 🍞 | Make some 🍞 |

#### Expletives
Strongly avoid curse words. Our primary audience does not respond well to profanity, and our Care team's Zendesk filter for expletives may cause applicable tickets to be missed.

### Writing for product

When writing for product, adjust voice and tone depending on audience (D2C = more playful; B2B = more bold and impactful). Unless otherwise noted, all UX writing uses sentence case.

#### UI element guidelines

**Buttons:** Limit to 3-5 words. Never go to two lines. Avoid pronouns. Format: "Would you like to [button copy]" or "I would like to [button copy]."

**Callouts:**
- Sticky: convey important textual information. Max 2 lines of body copy. Include closing punctuation.
- Dismissible: drive engagement for new or underused features. Generally no closing punctuation.

**Checkboxes, radios, and switches:** Always pair with a clear, concise label. For on/off switches, avoid saying "on" and "off" — label should make state clear.

**Chips:** Always include a headline and body copy. Body copy ≤ 2 lines or 50 characters. Don't repeat title copy in body copy.

**Confirmation dialogs:** In the form of a question (`Are you sure you want to [action]?`). Primary CTA: "Yes, [action]." Explain the action and its consequences.

**Empty states:** Include a headline and subheadline. Provide instructions for how to find content they're looking for.

**Error messages:**
- Never blame the user.
- Input field errors: max 2 lines; be clear about what's wrong.
- System/timing errors: "We couldn't/weren't able to…" Include "Try again" and "Cancel" CTAs.
- Messaging can start with "Uh oh" or "Hmm" to be more conversational.

**Form fields:** Clear, short labels. Leave placeholder text blank unless specific formatting is needed.

**Sheets and modals:** Include headline, body copy, and at least one action. Always include a way to dismiss. Body copy should be supplemental — not critical to the user's experience.

**Navigation:** Use title case. Categories are navigational elements — use title case and bold formatting when referencing in body copy.

**Toasts:** Max 2 lines. No headline. Close with a period. Can include a CTA to undo the action.

**Tooltips/info bubbles:** Avoid headline unless necessary. Use punctuation. Use a list if information is dense.
